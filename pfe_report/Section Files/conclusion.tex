\section{Conclusions and Future Work}

This chapter closes the report. It draws the conclusions of the work against the objectives and research questions stated in the introduction, then outlines directions in which the framework and the delivered platform could be extended.

\subsection{Conclusions}

This project addressed a concrete and pressing problem. The Tunisian banking sector, under the dual pressure of the BCT Circulaire N\textdegree{}2025-08 and the international BCBS 239 standard, had no standardized, rigorous, regulation aligned means of measuring and steering its data maturity. Existing maturity models were either too generic to capture the obligations of a supervised bank or too abstract to apply without extensive interpretation, and none combined a defensible measurement method with a tool management could operate directly. The work delivered a complete answer through two connected artifacts: a hybrid data maturity framework and the DataPilot tool that operationalizes it, both designed and validated through a Design Science Research methodology combined with an Agile, iteration based development process, here for methodological fidelity on simulated assessments, with real-bank field validation identified as future work.

The study met each of its five research objectives. The first, to diagnose the current state of the sector, was met through a diagnostic survey of 44 banking professionals, which established that data maturity is concentrated at the emerging level, Level 2 on the five level scale, and that the most significant gaps lie in the Governance and Data Quality dimensions. The second, to select an appropriate set of reference models, was met by analyzing fifteen candidate maturity frameworks against a common set of selection criteria; the five most relevant were retained, namely DAMA-DMBOK, DCAM, CMMI-DMM, TDWI, and Gartner, with an explicit record of what was retained and excluded from each. The third, to design a framework adapted to the sector, produced a hybrid structure of five dimensions, twelve sub-dimensions, and 47 evidence based indicators, with dimension weights of 25\%, 20\%, 20\%, 20\%, and 15\% for Governance, Data Quality, Architecture and Access, Analytics and Tools, and Skills and Culture respectively, and thirteen non-skippable regulatory indicators anchored in the BCT Circulaire N\textdegree{}2025-08 and the principles of BCBS 239. The fourth, to make the measurement auditable, was met by an aggregation method that averages indicator scores into sub-dimension scores, these into dimension scores, and these into a Global Maturity Index on a one to five scale, governed by an evidence cap that limits any unsubstantiated high score to two, a bounded skip logic that permits at most a fifth of a dimension's indicators to be deferred, and a rule forbidding the skipping of any of the thirteen regulatory indicators. The fifth, to deliver a usable instrument, was met by DataPilot itself, which delivers the six screen assessment workflow that automates the scoring and presents the maturity profile, the gap analysis, the three phase improvement roadmap, and the regulatory compliance view; in the final iteration it became a multi-tenant platform with four roles, EY Admin (owner), Super Admin, Admin, and Analyst, through which EY administers the instrument across banks and each bank conducts its own solo and group assessments. These five objectives together resolve, one by one, the four operational problems framed in Chapter 1: the absence of a structured reference framework, answered by the hybrid model; the lack of visibility on transformation priorities and their business gains, answered by the gap analysis, the target-level comparison, and the deterministic three phase roadmap that make the levers of improvement explicit and prioritized; the difficulty of positioning an institution against data maturity standards and regulatory requirements, answered by the thirteen non-skippable regulatory indicators and the dedicated compliance view that locate a bank against the BCT Circulaire N\textdegree{}2025-08 and BCBS 239; and the absence of an interactive decision-support tool, answered by DataPilot itself. That fourth problem also called for simulating the business benefits of a data transformation; this need is addressed today through the target maturity level, the visualization of the gap that separates the current profile from it, and the roadmap that sequences its closure, while the quantitative what-if simulation of business gains is the identified extension in the future work below, one the deterministic aggregation chain already makes straightforward to add.

The three research questions were answered correspondingly. The current state of data maturity in Tunisian banks was characterized, with its gaps relative to international standards and regulatory requirements made measurable and explicit rather than described in general terms. A framework adapted to the sector was designed by combining the five benchmark frameworks with the survey findings, each reference model contributing a distinct, non-overlapping role, from the knowledge area structure of DAMA-DMBOK to the maturity ladder of CMMI-DMM and the transformation lens of Gartner. And the capacity of an interactive steering tool to support strategic decision making was demonstrated through DataPilot, which converts a static assessment into an actionable instrument by automating the scoring, renormalizing the weights over the dimensions actually answered, prioritizing the gaps by severity, and exposing regulatory readiness through a dedicated compliance view. This capacity now extends to collaborative assessment at institutional scale, with responsibility for each dimension distributed to the department that holds its evidence and a coordinator consolidating the contributions into one auditable submission.

Beyond meeting its objectives, the project delivered three contributions of lasting value. The first is conceptual: a banking specific, regulation aligned hybrid framework that unifies five established reference models into a single coherent structure bound to the Tunisian supervisory context. The second is methodological: a rigorous, auditable evidence based scoring method in which the evidence cap, the skip limit, and the regulatory non-skippable rule together make a maturity score defensible before an auditor rather than merely indicative. The third is practical: an operational tool that bank management can use directly, without specialist intermediation, to obtain a maturity profile, a prioritized roadmap, and a compliance position in a single session, delivered as a multi-tenant platform in which EY maintains the master reference instrument, each bank tailors its own copy, and the four roles collaborate under database-enforced tenant isolation. The worked example in this report has a representative institution score a Global Maturity Index of 2.19 out of 5, equivalent to 44 percent and to the emerging level, illustrating how the three contributions turn a set of survey responses into an interpretable, actionable diagnosis.

Three external constraints shaped the work, in each case for the better. First, no direct instrument existed for measuring skills and culture, which forced the proxy only rule for the fifth dimension; the least observable dimension is thus explicitly labeled proxy based and deliberately bounded at 15\% of the index, rather than being silently unreliable. Second, the confidentiality expectations attached to banking data first forced a fully client-side architecture, in which every response, score, and evidence reference remained in the browser local storage of the assessing institution; when the final iteration's platform made a shared backend unavoidable, the same expectations forced the isolation discipline that replaced local custody, under which every row is confined to its own bank by row-level security, access is invite-only with no public sign-up, no user can escalate their own role, and submissions are immutable once recorded. The custody argument became a tenant isolation argument, and the constraint sharpened the design a second time. Third, the absence of access to production bank data confined the evaluation to simulated scenarios, concentrating it on the determinism and fidelity of the instrument, precisely the properties a future field pilot will inherit rather than re-establish. These constraints delimit the claims the report can make, but they also sharpened the design, and their honest documentation is itself part of the contribution. The recurring lesson, established in the discussion and worth restating, is that the score is trustworthy precisely where the method constrains it: the evidence cap, the skip ceilings, and the non-skippable regulatory rule are not restrictions on the instrument but the source of its defensibility.

\subsection{Future Work}

While the framework and the tool are complete and functional, several directions would extend their value and address the limitations acknowledged in this report. They are ordered roughly by immediacy, from the empirical validation that would consolidate the present results to the broader extensions that would enlarge the scope of the work. None require reworking the core artifacts; each builds on the structural model, the scoring methodology, and the tool as they stand.

\subsubsection{Validation on Real Institutional Data}

The tool was evaluated on simulated banking data rather than on a full production deployment inside a supervised institution. The most immediate priority is therefore to conduct pilot assessments with one or more real banks, ideally spanning institutions of different sizes and maturity levels so that the framework is exercised across the full range of the one to five scale. Such pilots would serve three purposes at once. They would calibrate the 47 indicators against field reality, confirming that each rubric level is distinguishable and that the evidence requirements are neither too lax nor too demanding for the documentation banks actually hold. They would confirm the usability of the questionnaire for bank staff who are not data specialists, in particular the clarity of the hints and the workability of the evidence cap when real evidence must be attached. And they would validate the relevance of the improvement roadmaps by checking that the actions surfaced for a low scoring dimension correspond to the steps practitioners would themselves prioritize. A structured validation workshop with governance officers, data owners, and compliance staff would then refine the rubrics, the weighting, and the author's mapping of indicators to the BCT Circulaire N\textdegree{}2025-08 and the principles of BCBS 239, closing the loop between the designed artifact and its intended field of use.

\subsubsection{Strengthening the Skills and Culture Dimension}

The fifth dimension, Skills and Culture, is currently assessed through proxy indicators only, since no direct self assessment instrument was available during the project and cultural attributes are intrinsically harder to observe than the documentary artifacts anchoring the other four dimensions. As a consequence it carries none of the thirteen regulatory indicators, and its eight indicators infer culture from measurable surrogates such as training budgets, the ratio of data roles to headcount, recent certifications, and communities of practice. Future work could develop a dedicated instrument, for example a structured staff survey or a data literacy assessment administered across business units, that measures the culture directly rather than approximating it through investment proxies. Combined with the existing proxy indicators, it would improve the dimension's construct validity, allow proxy and direct measures to be cross checked, and give the 15\% weight assigned to Skills and Culture a firmer empirical basis.

\subsubsection{Sector Level Benchmarking and Anonymized Comparison}

The framework produces a Global Maturity Index comparable across institutions by construction, since it uses the same dimensions, indicators, weights, and aggregation rules for every bank. A natural extension is therefore a sector benchmarking capability that lets each institution position itself against an anonymized sector distribution rather than an absolute scale alone. A bank could then see not only that it sits at the emerging level but where it stands relative to its peers on each dimension, which sharpens the interpretation of a score and helps management judge the urgency of a gap. The platform delivered in the final iteration now provides the collection mechanism this presupposed: every finalized assessment is recorded as an immutable submission in a central store EY can review across banks, leaving only the aggregation layer to build. Realizing it would still require a governance arrangement for aggregating results, with careful attention to anonymization so that no individual institution can be identified from the aggregates, ideally established in coordination with the regulator given its natural role as a trusted aggregator. Such an arrangement would transform the tool from an institutional instrument into a sector observatory, giving the supervisor a consolidated, evidence based view of data maturity across the banks it oversees.

\subsubsection{Scenario Simulation and Target Setting}

DataPilot already supports selecting a target maturity level against which the current profile is compared. A valuable extension would be a scenario simulation capability that estimates the effect of specific remediation actions on the Global Maturity Index before any is undertaken. Because the aggregation chain is fully deterministic, from indicator to sub-dimension to dimension to the weighted and renormalized index, the tool already contains everything needed to recompute a projected score when selected indicators are raised to a higher rubric level. Exposed as an interactive what if analysis, this would let management compare investment options by their projected impact on maturity and, just as importantly, on regulatory compliance, showing how many of the thirteen regulatory indicators would move from non compliant to compliant. Layered over successive assessments, it would let an institution plan a maturity trajectory over time and choose the sequence of actions that closes its regulatory gaps most efficiently.

\subsubsection{AI-Assisted Roadmap Generation}

The improvement roadmap is generated deterministically, by banding each dimension score and surfacing a curated set of recommended actions, which keeps every suggestion reproducible and auditable. The platform already includes an optional endpoint that can pass these actions through a language model to rephrase or enrich them for the specific institution, but it is disabled by default and was not enabled in the evaluated tool. Turning it on in production is deliberately left as future work: sending a bank's assessment content to an external model raises data-residency and confidentiality questions that must be resolved first, for example by using a model hosted within the bank's own jurisdiction or by restricting its input to non-sensitive dimension labels. In every case the generated text remains an optional presentation layer over the deterministic, auditable roadmap rather than the source of the recommendation itself.

\subsubsection{From Delivered Platform to Enterprise Integration}

The backend, the persistence of multiple assessments over time, and the multi-user collaboration that earlier stages could only list as future work were delivered by the fourth iteration, described in Chapter 4, and are recorded here as achievements rather than prospects. The platform stores every finalized result as an immutable submission in a central database, so an institution's assessments accumulate and successive maturity positions can be compared; the group assessment lets each department answer the indicators of its own dimensions within one shared evaluation, precisely the collaboration in which data owners answer their own domain; and the authorship of every group answer is recorded, giving the audit trail its first concrete form. What remains is the enterprise integration that would embed the platform in the daily infrastructure of a bank and the consulting practice, and four directions stand out. First, single sign-on and enterprise identity federation would connect the invite based accounts to the directories banks already operate, so that joiners, movers, and leavers are governed by the institution's existing identity lifecycle rather than manual invitations alone. Second, audit dashboards would surface the authorship and history the platform already records, letting an internal auditor see who answered each indicator of a group assessment, when a draft was finalized, and how an institution's submissions have evolved between assessments. Third, the sector benchmarking discussed above is now directly enabled by these accumulated submissions. Fourth, automated evidence checking would assist reviewers by flagging evidence texts that are empty, duplicated across indicators, or inconsistent with the claimed rubric level, extending to the review stage the discipline the evidence cap already imposes at scoring.

\subsubsection{Extension to Adjacent Sectors}

The framework is deliberately specific to the banking sector and the Tunisian regulatory environment, and this specificity is a source of its rigor. Its architecture, however, separates the structural model, the five dimensions, twelve sub-dimensions, and general purpose indicators, from the regulatory anchoring carried by the thirteen indicators tied to the BCT Circulaire N\textdegree{}2025-08 and BCBS 239. This separation makes the framework portable. Future work could adapt it to adjacent regulated sectors such as insurance by re-anchoring the regulatory indicators to the relevant supervisory texts, for example the obligations of the insurance regulator, while retaining the structural backbone, the scoring methodology with its evidence cap and skip logic, and the tool itself. The same approach would extend the framework to other jurisdictions by substituting the local banking regulation for the Tunisian circular. In each case the engineering effort would concentrate on the regulatory indicators and their references, leaving the bulk of the artifact, and the accumulated design rationale behind it, intact.

\subsection{Closing Remark}

This project demonstrates that the gap between a rigorous academic maturity model and a usable management instrument can be closed. The work grounds the framework in established research, anchors it in the applicable regulation, and delivers it through an operational tool and a governed platform through which an advisor can operate it across the sector. In doing so, it provides the Tunisian banking sector with both a measure of where it stands and a path toward where regulation and competition require it to be.
