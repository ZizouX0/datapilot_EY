\section{Results and Findings}

This chapter presents the consolidated results of the project and answers the research questions. It first reports the findings of the sector diagnostic, then summarizes the resulting maturity profile, then explains how that profile is translated into a prioritized improvement roadmap, and finally provides an explicit answer to each of the three research questions. The results are organized so that empirical observation, framework output, and actionable guidance are clearly separated. The diagnostic findings describe the observed state of the sector; the maturity profile expresses that state on the common one to five scale of the framework. The roadmap converts the gaps into ordered actions, and the answers to the research questions close the loop back to the objectives stated in the introduction.

Two clarifications frame the interpretation of these results. First, the diagnostic survey and the assessment framework play complementary but distinct roles. The survey supplies aggregate sector evidence that calibrates the framework and validates its relevance, whereas the framework and the DataPilot tool are the instruments through which any single institution is positioned and steered. The representative maturity profile discussed below is therefore an illustrative synthesis, consistent with the survey evidence, rather than the score of one named bank. Second, all quantitative results are expressed through the scoring methodology defined in the design iterations. Every figure reported here therefore traces back to the same auditable aggregation chain, the evidence cap, the skip logic, and the weighted Global Maturity Index.

A final clarification concerns the boundary of what these results establish, so that the figures below are not read as more than they are. The evaluation behind them is functional testing on simulated data: complete assessment sessions were entered into the tool and their outputs checked against the methodology. What such testing establishes is that the instrument is faithful. Every displayed index, band, and classification can be retraced by hand from the stored answers through the published rules, and the inputs an assessor provides are converted into scores exactly as the methodology prescribes. What it does not establish is that a field audit has occurred. No figure in this chapter is the measured score of a production deployment in a named bank, and no claim is made about a population parameter beyond what the diagnostic survey itself supports. The interpretation of this boundary, and the threats to validity that follow from it, are deferred to the Discussion chapter, where they are examined in full.

\subsection{Sector Diagnostic Findings}

The diagnostic survey was distributed online to individual banking professionals and gathered 44 responses. The responses are anonymous, and a single institution may be represented by more than one respondent. All percentages below are therefore reported at the level of respondents rather than of distinct institutions, and no claim is made about the number of banks behind the sample. In governance, only 30\% of respondents reported a formally approved data strategy. In data quality, 56.8\% of respondents verify data before use, and most rely on manual checks rather than automated validation. In architecture and access, 54\% of respondents described access to data as slow, and 40.9\% reported that data is only partially centralized. In analytics and tools, 41\% rated their tools as sufficient but limited, while 36\% described their decisions as data driven against 50\% still relying on intuition. The fifth dimension, skills and culture, could not be assessed directly and was approached through proxy indicators.

\subsubsection{Governance}

The governance findings are the most consequential of the diagnostic, because governance carries the highest weight in the index, at 25\%, and because it concentrates the largest share of the non-skippable regulatory indicators. The observation that only 30\% of respondents reported a formally approved data strategy speaks directly to sub-dimension 1.1, strategy and data policy. In particular, it speaks to the indicators that ask for a formally approved and dated strategy document and for a periodic review process for the data policy. Where the strategy layer is absent or informal, the downstream governance indicators covering ownership, stewardship, and accountability chains rarely reach a mature level, because there is no mandated authority to assign and enforce them. The consequence is a governance dimension whose observed strength sits at the lower part of the emerging band. The regulatory sub-dimension 1.3, regulatory compliance, is exposed to the requirements of the BCT Circulaire N\textdegree{}2025-08 and to the BCBS 239 traceability expectations for risk reporting.

\subsubsection{Data Quality}

In data quality, the finding that 56.8\% of respondents verify data before use is best read as evidence of awareness rather than of institutionalized control. Verification before use is a manual, individual behavior, whereas the framework rewards automated data validation rules documented and applied in production, tracked quality incident rates, and formalized remediation. The prevalence of manual checks over automated validation therefore places the dimension at the emerging level: the intent to ensure quality is present, but it is not yet embedded in repeatable processes or in operational dashboards that drive decisions. Sub-dimension 2.3, lineage and traceability, is particularly relevant here, because end-to-end lineage for critical data flows is both a quality control and a regulatory requirement, and its absence weakens the auditability that BCBS 239 demands for risk reporting.

\subsubsection{Architecture and Access}

The architecture and access findings show partial progress. That 54\% of respondents described access to data as slow, and 40.9\% reported only partial centralization, points to an architecture that is transitioning rather than stalled. Partial centralization means that some business lines have been integrated while data silos persist elsewhere, which is consistent with an emerging dimension that is beginning to climb. The regulatory indicators in this dimension are the single source of truth or golden record for critical entities and role-based access control with complete audit trails. These indicators are the levers that most improve both the architecture score and the compliance posture, because they simultaneously reduce fragmentation and satisfy supervisory expectations.

\subsubsection{Analytics and Tools}

In analytics and tools, 41\% of respondents rated their tools as sufficient but limited, while 36\% described their decisions as data driven against 50\% still relying on intuition. Combined, these findings characterize a dimension where capability exists but is not yet the default basis for decision-making. The tools are present, yet self-service analytics, automation of key workflows, and, above all, the automation of regulatory reporting to the BCT with validation trails remain incomplete. This gap between available tooling and actual data-driven practice is the defining feature of the emerging level in this dimension. The infrastructure to be effective is partly in place, but the behaviors and the automation that would make it managed are not.

\subsubsection{Skills and Culture}

The fifth dimension, skills and culture, could not be assessed directly through the survey instrument and is therefore treated as a proxy dimension in the framework. It is assessed only through proxy indicators such as the ratio of data roles to total headcount, data-specific roles hired in the past twelve months, an active change management program, and operational communities of practice. This design choice is deliberate: rather than fabricate a direct measurement that the instrument cannot support, the framework marks the dimension as a proxy and weights it at 15\%, the lowest of the five. In this way, its inherent uncertainty is bounded and clearly labelled to the assessor. The proxy status is a limitation to be acknowledged rather than concealed, and it is one of the priorities identified for future refinement of the instrument.

The composition in Table~\ref{tab:profile-breakdown} makes the aggregation explicit. Each dimension score is multiplied by its weight, and because all five dimensions are answered in this example, the weights already sum to 100\% and no renormalization is required. The weighted contributions, 0.480, 0.416, 0.476, 0.500, and 0.320, add to 2.19, which the percentage conversion turns into round(2.19 times 20) equal to 44\%. The value 2.19 falls inside the 1.80 to 2.59 band (the maturity bands are defined in Appendix~E, with the underlying level rubrics in Appendix~A), so the institution is placed at Level 2, Emerging. Were one dimension left unanswered, its weight would be removed from the denominator and the remaining weights rescaled to sum to 100\%, so that the index always reflects only the dimensions that were actually assessed. This renormalization is what allows a partial assessment to remain meaningful without silently treating a missing dimension as a score of zero. The reported maturity level is, moreover, not sensitive to the exact weighting. Suppose the five dimension weights are each shifted by up to five percentage points around their nominal values of 25/20/20/20/15 and renormalized to sum to one hundred percent. The Global Maturity Index of this representative profile then varies only between approximately 2.14 and 2.24. Under equal weights it is 2.20. Every one of these values falls well inside the Level 2 Emerging band. The weighting therefore fixes the emphasis of the index and the relative ordering of the dimensions, while the reported level is robust to the precise choice of weights.

The profile also illustrates the notion of a critical gap. Governance, at 1.92 out of 5, sits below the threshold of 2.0 and is therefore flagged as a critical gap by the framework. A dimension score under 2.0 signals a weakness serious enough to require priority attention regardless of the overall index. This 2.0 gap threshold is deliberately distinct from the 1.80 lower bound of the Level 2 band. The band labels the overall maturity level, whereas the gap threshold flags a single dimension for priority action. A dimension can therefore sit inside the Emerging band and still be flagged as a critical gap, as governance is here. Governance is the only dimension flagged, since every other dimension score sits above 2.0. Data quality, at 2.08, is the second weakest dimension and, sitting just above the gap threshold, is not flagged, but it is the natural next target once the governance gap is addressed. Analytics, at 2.50, and architecture, at 2.38, are the relative strengths of the profile, though they too remain within the emerging band and well short of the defined level.

Taken together, the findings describe a sector whose maturity is concentrated at the emerging level. Governance and data quality are the weakest dimensions, held back by the absence of formal strategy and automated control, while architecture and analytics show partial progress. This profile establishes the baseline against which individual institutions can be positioned using the framework and the DataPilot tool, and against which improvement can be steered.

\subsection{From Profile to Improvement Roadmap}

The value of the maturity profile is realized only when it is converted into action. DataPilot performs this conversion through its gap analysis, which operates at the level of the twelve sub-dimensions rather than the five dimensions, so that guidance is targeted at the specific practice that is weak rather than at a whole dimension. Each sub-dimension is assigned a priority on a deterministic four-level scale that mirrors the scoring thresholds: critical below 2.0, high below 2.6, moderate below 3.4, and low at 3.4 and above. These four priorities are then grouped into a three-phase, time-sequenced roadmap. The grouping is not a simple copy of the priority label, because regulatory risk is allowed to override it. A sub-dimension that contains a failing regulatory indicator, or whose score is below 2.0, is placed in the first phase regardless of its numeric priority. The remaining sub-dimensions that still fall short of the defined level go to the second phase, and any sub-dimension that has reached the defined level goes to the third. Each sub-dimension card surfaces a small, bounded set of concrete actions, so the roadmap is specific rather than open-ended.

Applied to the representative profile, this logic yields a clear sequencing, shown in the gap analysis screen of Chapter~4. Five sub-dimensions fall in the first phase. The first two are the weakest governance sub-dimensions, strategy and data policy and ownership and responsibilities, both below 2.0. The third is the governance regulatory-compliance sub-dimension, which is pulled into the first phase not by its score but because it still carries a failing regulatory indicator, namely audit readiness. The last two are the data-quality controls and processes sub-dimension, below 2.0, and the data-skills proxy sub-dimension, also below 2.0. Their first-phase actions include formally approving a data strategy document at board level and appointing a chief data officer or equivalent with an executive mandate. They also include establishing a data governance committee with a formal charter and closing the two open regulatory gaps in governance. The remaining seven sub-dimensions, all sitting between 2.0 and 3.0 in this profile, fall in the second phase. Their actions include steps such as automating key data quality controls in critical pipelines, integrating all major business lines into a centralized platform, documenting end-to-end lineage, and launching self-service analytics with user training. The third phase is empty for this profile, because no sub-dimension has yet reached the defined level. The optimization actions associated with it, such as introducing predictive quality monitoring or measuring data culture adoption through an annual index, are therefore staged for later. They become relevant once a sub-dimension crosses into the defined level.

The three phases therefore correspond to increasing levels of maturity and to a time horizon. The first phase, over the first three months, remediates the critical gaps and the open regulatory items and establishes the missing foundations. The second phase, over three to six months, automates and integrates the partially mature sub-dimensions, and the third phase, over six to twelve months, optimizes and benchmarks the sub-dimensions that have reached the defined level. Figure~\ref{fig:roadmap} presents this three-phase model and the representative action themes attached to each phase.

The roadmap is not merely a presentation device. The banding is derived directly from the same scores that produce the index, so the sequence of actions is auditable and reproducible. Two assessors who enter the same raw scores will obtain the same bands and therefore the same ordered actions. This is a statement about computational reproducibility, not about inter-rater agreement, since two assessors may still read the same rubric and evidence and enter different raw scores; that distinction is examined in the Discussion. This determinism is what makes the roadmap a steering instrument rather than a consultative opinion. It is also what allows an institution to re-run the assessment after an intervention and observe the resulting movement of a dimension out of the critical band and toward the defined level.

\subsection{An Assessment Session End to End}

This walkthrough follows the solo assessment path as delivered in the single-user build of Iteration 3, whose screens are the captures shown in Chapter 4. In the platform of Iteration 4, the session identity is supplied by the analyst's invited account rather than typed into a form, and a completed solo assessment can additionally be submitted for review to the bank's administrators. The multi-tenant flows, including the collaborative group assessment, are specified in Iteration 4. The results presented above are produced through one continuous workflow, and following a complete session from the assessor's seat shows how the instrument behaves at the moments where its rules matter, tracing the journey summarized in Figure~\ref{fig:user-journey}. A session opens on the Welcome screen, where the assessment cannot begin until the inline profile form, covering the bank name, the date, the respondent name, and the role, is complete and the start action becomes available. The questionnaire then presents the forty seven indicators grouped by dimension and sub-dimension, beginning with the twelve indicators of governance. For each indicator, the assessor reads the question and the five level rubric, selects a score, and documents the supporting evidence. The business rules act at the moment of entry rather than at the end. An indicator scored four with its evidence field left empty is immediately computed and displayed as a two and flagged as capped. The skip control is disabled on the thirteen regulatory indicators, and a request to skip a third governance indicator is refused, the ceiling for that dimension being two. Only when the forty seventh indicator has been answered or lawfully skipped does the completion gate open the results, gap analysis, and compliance views, which until then redirect back to the questionnaire. Figures~\ref{fig:screen-questionnaire} through~\ref{fig:screen-compliance} in Chapter 4 show these screens as captured from a complete demonstration session.

The representative Level 2 institution whose composition is given in Table~\ref{tab:profile-breakdown} is the same session captured in the DataPilot screenshots of Chapter 4, so the figures discussed here can be read directly off those screens. The results dashboard displays the Global Maturity Index of 2.19 out of 5, its percentage form of 44\%, and the Level 2 Emerging badge. It also displays the radar chart overlaying the five dimension scores of 1.92, 2.08, 2.38, 2.50, and 2.13 on the selected target level. The gap analysis reads the same stored scores and flags governance, at 1.92, as the single critical gap of the profile. It places the two weakest governance sub-dimensions and the still-open governance regulatory items in the first phase of the roadmap, alongside the other sub-dimensions that are below 2.0, while the remaining sub-dimensions fall into the second phase. Nothing on these screens is computed anew: every figure the assessor sees is a projection of the same stored answers through the aggregation chain of the design iterations. This is what allows any displayed number to be retraced by hand (Appendix~E).

The compliance view closes the session with a classification of the regulatory core, and its mechanics can be made concrete on the same session, shown in the Results and Compliance screenshots of Chapter 4, Figures~\ref{fig:screen-results} and~\ref{fig:screen-compliance}. The rule is the one defined in the design iterations. Each of the thirteen regulatory indicators is classified from its effective score: compliant at three out of five or above, non-compliant below that threshold, and pending when unanswered. The compliant threshold sits at three because level three is the point at which an obligation is formalized, documented, and demonstrable rather than merely intended. The compliance rate is the compliant share of the thirteen, rounded to a percentage. The exposure is rated low from a rate of 80\% upward, medium from 50\% upward, and high below that, exactly as set out in Appendix~E. These exposure bands are an operational classification chosen to give management a simple risk signal; they are not defined by the regulator. An institution using the instrument for a formal compliance exercise would treat any non-compliant regulatory indicator as requiring remediation regardless of the aggregate rate. In the captured session, ten of the thirteen regulatory indicators reach an effective score of three and are classified as compliant. The remaining three, each at an effective score of two and all located in the governance dimension, are non-compliant. None is pending, since the session is complete. The rate is therefore round(10/13 times 100) equal to 77\%, and because 77\% lies between the 50\% and 80\% thresholds, the regulatory exposure is rated medium. This session, whose profile is given in Table~\ref{tab:profile-breakdown}, sits within the emerging band, and its summary shows what the compliance view adds to the index. An institution can be three indicators away from a low exposure rating and know exactly which three. Each non-compliant line names its indicator, its dimension, its regulatory reference, and its supporting evidence.

\subsection{Answers to the Research Questions}

Drawing on the diagnostic findings, the resulting maturity profile, and the artifacts presented above, this section answers each of the three research questions in turn, together with its sub-questions.

\subsubsection{RQ1 Answer}

\textbf{In short: the sector sits at the emerging level (Level 2), with governance and data quality the weakest and most exposed areas.} The current state of data maturity in Tunisian banks is concentrated at the emerging level, with the main gaps relative to international standards and regulatory requirements concentrated in governance and data quality. Most institutions lack a formally approved data strategy and rely on manual rather than automated quality control, which places them below the governance and control expectations of the BCT Circulaire N\textdegree{}2025-08 and BCBS 239. The diagnostic and the framework together make these gaps measurable and explicit.

In answer to RQ1a, the diagnostic evidence locates these gaps dimension by dimension. Governance is the weakest area, because the strategy layer that should mandate ownership, stewardship, and regulatory alignment is reported in place by only a minority of respondents. Data quality is the second weakest, because verification remains a manual behavior rather than an automated and monitored control, which is precisely where BCBS 239 and the reliability of regulatory reporting are most sensitive. Architecture and analytics are further along, showing partial centralization and partly sufficient tooling, but neither reaches the defined level, and both are held back by incomplete automation and residual data silos. Skills and culture cannot be measured directly and is inferred through proxy indicators. In answer to RQ1b, the survey evidence indicates that non-compliance concentrates on the governance obligations, the formally approved data strategy and audit readiness in particular. At the level of a single institution, the instrument quantifies the compliance rate over the thirteen non-skippable indicators and the exposure that rate implies. The diagnostic survey itself does not yield a sector-wide regulatory compliance rate. The 77\% rate and the resulting Medium exposure reported here are for the single demonstration session only, and are not a sector result. Establishing a sector-wide compliance rate would therefore require the field pilot assessments identified in the future work. The overall picture is a sector that is aware of the importance of data and has begun to invest, but that has not yet institutionalized the strategy, control, and automation that would move it from emerging to defined. The gaps are not diffuse impressions but specific, indicator-level deficiencies that the framework can locate and the tool can track.

\subsubsection{RQ2 Answer}

\textbf{In short: a hybrid framework was designed that fuses five established benchmarks into five dimensions, twelve sub-dimensions, and forty seven indicators, scored by an evidence-capped weighted Global Maturity Index.} A data maturity assessment framework adapted to the Tunisian banking sector was designed by combining established benchmarks with the survey findings. In answer to RQ2a, the comparative analysis showed that each of the five frameworks contributes a distinct strength: DAMA-DMBOK its structure, DCAM its regulatory alignment, CMMI its scale, TDWI its evaluation logic, and Gartner its executive communication. It also showed that no single framework is sufficient alone. In answer to RQ2b, the framework was structured around five dimensions, twelve sub-dimensions, and forty seven indicators. It carries a five level maturity scale and a weighted scoring methodology that produces an auditable Global Maturity Index (GMI) on a one to five scale, informed by the survey findings.

The adaptation to the local context is what distinguishes the framework from a direct import of any single benchmark. The five dimensions are weighted at 25\%, 20\%, 20\%, 20\%, and 15\%, giving governance the largest influence in recognition of its regulatory centrality in the Tunisian banking sector. Thirteen of the forty seven indicators are flagged as regulatory and non-skippable, and they are mapped to specific requirements of the BCT Circulaire N\textdegree{}2025-08 and to the BCBS 239 principles. Regulatory readiness is therefore measured within the same instrument that measures maturity rather than as a separate exercise. The methodology further embeds an evidence cap, which prevents an unsupported high score from inflating the result, and a bounded skip mechanism that excludes the skipped indicators from the denominators while forbidding any skip of a regulatory indicator. The result is a framework that is at once standard-aligned, locally adapted, and auditable, which is what the research question asked for.

\subsubsection{RQ3 Answer}

\textbf{In short: DataPilot operationalizes the framework into auditable automated scoring, dashboards, a prioritized improvement roadmap, and a regulatory compliance view.} The interactive tool supports strategic decision-making by transforming a static assessment into an actionable instrument. DataPilot automates the scoring, presents the maturity profile through dashboards, prioritizes improvement actions through a gap analysis, and reports regulatory readiness through a compliance view. By making the maturity state and the improvement path explicit and auditable, the tool gives bank management a concrete basis on which to steer their data strategy and to accelerate their digital transformation.

Concretely, the tool closes the distance between assessment and action along four axes. In answer to RQ3a, it removes manual computation error by applying the aggregation chain, the evidence cap, the renormalization, and the percentage conversion consistently. The reported index is therefore always reproducible, resistant to over-optimistic self-assessment, and, when the same set of dimensions is assessed, comparable across institutions. It makes the profile legible to non-specialists by rendering the per-dimension scores as a radar chart and a set of dimension bars, and by attaching a maturity badge that names the level. In answer to RQ3b, it converts the profile into a sequenced roadmap by banding each dimension score and surfacing the corresponding actions, so that management is given an ordered set of interventions rather than a raw diagnosis. And it exposes regulatory exposure directly, by evaluating the thirteen regulatory indicators, reporting a compliance rate, and classifying the residual exposure as low, medium, or high. Because all of this is derived from a single stored set of answers and recomputed on demand, the tool also supports the re-assessment cycle that turns a one-time diagnosis into ongoing steering. In this sense the instrument provides the means to support decision-making and to accelerate the digital transformation. Demonstrating that a real institution's transformation was in fact accelerated would require the field pilot identified in the future work, and no such claim is drawn from the functional evaluation reported here. The platform delivered in Iteration 4 extends this support to the scale of the institution. Departments contribute the dimensions assigned to them within a group assessment, a coordinator consolidates their work into one auditable submission per bank, and EY reviews submissions across banks. The same instrument thus steers the transformation of a whole organization rather than a single assessor's session.

\subsection{Conclusion}

This chapter reported the sector diagnostic findings dimension by dimension, summarized the resulting maturity profile and its composition into the Global Maturity Index, explained how that profile is translated into a three-phase improvement roadmap, and answered the three research questions. The next chapter discusses these results, their implications, and the threats to validity.
