## Part 2 · The five hard jurors

## Juror 1 — The harsh methodology professor

**Verdict:** A well-engineered artifact wrapped in unusually honest limitation statements, but as research it is verification masquerading as validation: nothing in this thesis has measured anything real, and every external anchor passed through the author's own filter.

**What they would say to you:**

> Mr. Dardouri, I will grant you this: your limitations chapter is more honest than most, and your appendix anticipates half of my objections, which tells me you understand where the bodies are buried. But candor about a weakness does not repair it. Your thesis rests on a survey your own methodology declares untrustworthy, a 'validation' that consists of your tool reproducing rules you wrote from data you seeded, and a legal compliance module built on your personal reading of a circular that no compliance authority has seen. What you have built is a promising, auditable instrument; what you have not done, anywhere in 63 slides, is measure a single real thing — so every empirical-sounding claim in this deck, from 'the sector sits at Level 2' to '77% compliant', is at best a hypothesis and at worst decoration.

**Your weakest points in their eyes:**

- The epistemic core is circular: the sector diagnosis (RQ1) rests on 44 uncapped anonymous self-reports — the exact evidence type the thesis's own evidence-cap argument declares untrustworthy — and the author alone converted those percentages into a maturity band via a rubric he also wrote.
- 'Validated' in RQ2 means only functional fidelity: the tool reproducing the author's own rules on author-seeded simulated data, with no automated test suite, no inter-rater reliability measurement, no field data, and no independent reviewer at any step.
- Slide A10 contradicts the thesis: the deck promises silence can never bury a weakness, while the Discussion chapter admits a group coordinator can raise the index by leaving the weakest dimension unmapped, with enforcement of full coverage only listed as future work.
- The compliance module ('proves regulatory readiness line by line', 77% = Medium exposure) rests on the author's personal, unendorsed legal mapping plus invented thresholds (compliant at ≥3; 80/50 bands), and averages binding legal obligations in a way no regulator would — the single most overclaimed slide in the deck.
- The evidence cap verifies citation, not evidence: any attachment unlocks scores of 3–5, so the flagship anti-gaming mechanism is a deterrent and audit trail, not a control — a distinction the main deck blurs.
- Every 'external anchor' is self-filtered: the author chose the 15 candidates, invented and self-scored the 5 screening criteria, overrode the matrix 'on principle' (McKinsey), and set the weights — the 44-respondent survey is the only input he did not author, and it is the weakest evidence in the thesis.
- The ±5-point weight-robustness claim (C4/C3) is asserted on a slide but no sensitivity analysis is shown anywhere in the deck; the defensible convex-combination argument exists but the candidate never makes it.
- Headline slide 43 ('the sector's first defensible maturity yardstick') outruns the evidence: with zero real-bank assessments, defensibility is a design property, not a demonstrated one — the report's own careful phrasing ('built so that an audit would succeed, not already audited') is more honest than the deck's.

**Their hardest questions — and your best answers:**

**Q1. Your entire justification for the evidence cap is that self-assessment is optimistic and cannot be trusted (slide 26). Yet your answer to RQ1 — 'the Tunisian banking sector sits at Level 2' — rests exclusively on 44 anonymous, online, uncapped self-reports, with no evidence requirement, unknown response rate, unknown number of institutions, and possibly several respondents from the same bank. Why should the jury accept a sector diagnosis produced by exactly the method your own thesis was built to invalidate?**

*Why it's hard:* It turns the candidate's central design argument against his central empirical claim: the two pillars of the thesis (the diagnostic and the instrument) are epistemically at war with each other. Slide 30's wording ('forty-four professionals cluster at Level 2') also overstates what the report itself claims.

*Your answer:* Concede the asymmetry and separate the two claims, as the report's External Validity section does explicitly: the survey is labeled indicative and directional, reported at respondent level with no claim about the number of banks, and it plays a different role — it motivates the problem and calibrates the framework's relevance; it is not a measurement. The instrument's validity for any given bank rests on evidence collected for that bank, not on the survey. The Level 2 reading is triangulated across three independent signals (only 30% have an approved strategy, 57% manually re-verify data, 54% face slow access) that all fall in the same band, and the weakness of self-report is precisely why the deliverable is an evidence-capped instrument. The candidate should also soften slide 30's phrasing on the spot: the survey supports 'directional evidence of an Emerging sector', not a clustered score.

**Q2. The survey never produced a 1-to-5 score, yet somewhere '30% have a data strategy' became 'Level 2, GMI-band Emerging'. Who performed that mapping? You did — the same person whose framework is being evaluated. Isn't RQ1's answer simply your prior, restated through your own rubric, and the 2.19 worked example a number you chose to be consistent with the conclusion you had already drawn?**

*Why it's hard:* It exposes the circularity at the joint between the survey and the framework: the 'representative profile' is admitted in results.tex to be an 'illustrative synthesis consistent with the survey', i.e., authored, not observed. *Your answer:* Own the construction openly, as backup slide A6 does: 2.19 is a worked example on simulated data whose only function is functional fidelity — proving the engine computes end to end and every figure retraces by hand; it is a demonstration of the instrument, not a finding. **Q3. Slide 33 states RQ2 is answered because the framework was 'designed and validated'. *Your answer:* Concede the terminology and restate the boundary the report itself draws: what is established is design validation in the DSR sense — the artifact demonstrably meets its stated requirements — plus computational reproducibility (identical inputs give identical outputs, so the index is usable as a tracked indicator). **Q4. Your evidence cap checks that evidence is cited, not that it is genuine, relevant, or sufficient — the tool cannot open the document. On top of that self-declared basis, your compliance screen announces '10 of 13 regulatory indicators pass — 77%, Medium exposure'. *Why it's hard:* It is a direct, documented contradiction between the defense deck and the ground-truth report — the worst position for a candidate, because it suggests the presentation polishes over a known integrity hole in the flagship 'auditable' scoring engine.

*Your answer:* The only viable move is immediate, precise concession: the thesis is the authoritative text. A10's guarantee holds fully at indicator level (regulatory indicators non-skippable, unanswered indicators flagged to the admin) but not yet at whole-dimension level in group mode — an official finalized submission can currently omit a dimension, and the report itself identifies mandatory five-dimension coverage at finalization as a named hardening step. The mitigation today is procedural, not technical: the omission is visible in the coverage tracking (it does not disappear silently) and the finalized submission is immutable, so the gap is detectable by the EY reviewer even though the platform does not yet block it. The candidate should thank the jury for the catch and commit to correcting the slide.

**Q6. You selected the 15 candidate frameworks, scored the 15×5 screening matrix yourself on 1–3 scales of your own design, set the 25/20/20/20/15 weights yourself, and then claim the maturity band is 'robust to ±5 points'. Show me that sensitivity analysis — which weights were perturbed, jointly or singly, under what renormalization? *Why it's hard:* It combines the researcher-degrees-of-freedom attack (every 'systematic' step was self-scored, and 'principled, not arithmetic' selection on A14 reads as post-hoc licence to override the matrix) with a robustness claim (C3) that the deck asserts but never demonstrates, plus a parsimony challenge to the contribution's novelty.

The gap the question exposes is real, though, and it is precisely why the assessments are designed to be EY-administered rather than pure self-service, and why human evidence review is part of the operating model, not the software.

**Q4. Your scale goes from 1 to 5, and you convert by multiplying by 20. So a bank that scores the worst possible mark on every single indicator still gets 20%. What does '44%' actually mean, then? 44% of what?**

*Why it's hard:* It is grade-school arithmetic aimed straight at the deck's headline number — the big yellow '44%' hero stat. A9 rehearses 'false precision' on the decimals but nobody rehearsed the floor of the percentage scale: score×20 maps the 1–5 range onto 20–100%, so the percentage is not a fraction of anything real. Watching the candidate realize mid-answer that 44% overstates position-in-range (2.19 is actually ~30% of the way from worst to best) is exactly the unraveling this question is built for.

*Your answer:* You are right, and I should be precise: 44% is a display convention — score divided by the scale maximum — inherited from how CMMI-style indices are conventionally reported, not a fraction of achieved maturity above the floor; on a 'distance from worst to best' reading, 2.19 is about 30%. That is exactly why no decision in DataPilot is ever made on the percentage: the operative output is the discrete band — 2.19 falls in 1.80–2.59, Level 2 Emerging — and the bands are defined on the raw 1–5 scale, so the ×20 convention cannot change any bank's level, priority ranking, or compliance result. The percentage exists only because executives read '44%' faster than '2.19/5'; if the jury prefers, the display could be switched to a (score−1)/4 normalization in one line of the presentation layer without touching a single stored score — that is what a configurable, auditable engine buys you.

**Q5. Your survey says the sector is Level 2. Your 'simulated bank' — data you invented yourself — also lands at Level 2, with governance weakest, exactly matching your story. If you invented the inputs, what did the 2.19 actually test? Wouldn't any inputs have 'proved the engine computes end to end'?**

*Why it's hard:* It fuses two rehearsed answers (A6 'not a real bank' and A12 'functional fidelity') into a contradiction neither covers: if the point was only to prove arithmetic, the realism is irrelevant; if the realism matters, then the author fabricated data to confirm his own survey and then presented the agreement ('a clear, uncomfortable answer... lands squarely on Governance') as if it were a finding. *Your answer:* Correct — any inputs would have tested the arithmetic, and I claim nothing more from the 2.19 than functional fidelity: every rule of the engine (evidence cap, bounded skip, renormalization, weighting, compliance banding) fires on a case I can retrace by hand in front of you, which is the DSR design-validation step, not an empirical result. The profile was deliberately seeded to be survey-realistic for a different reason: a jury and future users need to see the dashboards, gap ranking and roadmap behave sensibly on a plausible bank, not on random noise. So the agreement with the survey is by construction, and I flagged it as such on the slide itself — 'a worked-example figure, not a sector-wide rate'. The only empirical claim I make, sector at Level 2, rests solely on the 44 respondents; the only methodological claim, the engine computes correctly and auditably, rests solely on the worked example. **Q6. You told us the score is 'comparable across banks'. But Bank A can skip up to 20% of indicators and Bank B different ones, and then you renormalize each differently. If both print 44%, are they equally mature — yes or no?**

*Why it's hard:* It sets two of the deck's own selling points against each other: RQ3 promises comparability, A3/A10 promise flexible skipping and renormalization — and you cannot maximize both. The honest answer is 'no, not exactly', which sounds like the headline promise breaking; the rehearsed A3 answer only defends skipping against abuse, not against incomparability. It also foreshadows the cross-bank benchmarking future-work claim, which quietly assumes the comparability being questioned.

*Your answer:* Not automatically, and the tool never hides that: every skip is a recorded, visible part of the assessment, so any two reports disclose exactly which indicator basis produced each number — comparability is achieved by transparency of basis, not by pretending the bases are identical. Three structural guarantees limit the drift: the skip is capped at 20% per dimension so at least 80% of every dimension is always common ground; all banks assess against the same EY master template, so the indicator set itself never varies; and the 13 regulatory indicators can never be skipped, which means the compliance view — the number the regulator actually acts on — is computed on a strictly identical basis for every bank, always 13 out of 13. So the GMI is comparable with disclosed caveats, and the regulatory comparison is exact by construction. For the future cross-bank benchmarking, the design answer is to benchmark on the common non-skipped core and the compliance rate, which is precisely why that layer is proposed for the regulator to run.
